He used to sit by the window
where the late sun softened everything.
He’d talk like someone trying to stay afloat,
voice thin, hands restless,
as if the air itself weighed too much.
He said he felt “transparent.”
I didn’t understand then
how close that was to disappearing.

He trusted her more than sleep.
Held her name like a fragile thing.
Saved every message she sent,
even the ones that said nothing.
He waited for her as if waiting were a job
and devotion could earn him a place
in a heart, already leaving.

She had already gone.
Already choosing someone else’s warmth.
Already rehearsing her excuses
in bathrooms and backseats.
Already building a world
where he was an afterthought,
a placeholder,
a soft thing she could step over.

When he found out,
he didn’t break.
He folded.
Quiet, slow, inward —
like a star collapsing
without light or sound.
He whispered,
“I guess I wasn’t enough,”
and I swear the room dimmed.

A week later,
his mother called me.
Her breath trembled
like something fragile in her hands.
I knew before she spoke.
I knew before she cried.
I knew because he had already
been fading in front of me.

And now—
You two walk around untouched,
as if you didn’t hollow him out.
As if you didn’t watch him unravel
and call it “bad timing.”
As if your hands aren’t stained
with the quiet way he died.

You ain’t love.
You ain’t loyalty.
You’re two mouths feeding
on whatever softness you can steal.
Two empty chests pretending
you know anything about devotion.
You talk about “chemistry.”
I call it cowardice with perfume.
You talk about “connection.”
I call it two liars
who found comfort in each other’s rot.
You talk about “mistakes.”
I call it the truth finally showing its face.

You ain’t guilt.
You ain’t grief.
You’re the echo of promises
you never meant to keep.
You’re the kind of people
who ruin gentle souls
and then blame the weather.

And him—
he deserved a world
that didn’t swallow him whole.
He deserved a love
that didn’t turn into a weapon.
He deserved to stay.
He deserved to breathe.
He deserved more than the two of you
ever had the capacity to give.

But you—
You’re the kind of filth
that calls itself complicated.
The kind of cheap hearts
that mistake desire for depth.
The kind of liars
who think their mess is poetry.
You ain’t tragic.
You ain’t misunderstood.
You’re just cheap.
Cheap souls, cheap loyalty,
cheap excuses dressed as fate.
And if there’s any justice left,
you’ll choke on the taste
of what you did to him —
every lie, every touch, every secret —
until it rots in your death.
